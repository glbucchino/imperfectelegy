An imperfect elegy.
A poem assembled for a boy who died.

He was snatched away,
hit on the tracks in the middle of a warm July night while the aimless fires of youth
burned inside of him.

This is an ode to a boy playing out the stories he read.
He was reckless and invincible,
a childhood spent inside a sunlit room surrounded by stacks of novels
with pages full of life being lived.
How many times had the image of a young man
tight rope walking on the tracks been shown to him?
He watched it and read it,
heard the tales from braver kids.

He was twenty one when the train emerged from the darkness at a speed he couldnâ€™t register.
How fast does a train that kills a boy on the tracks go in the night?
How bright are the headlights when he meets their gaze?
How long is he a boy before he is dead?

I was told the next morning on the phone, 3,000 miles away.
His death echoed throughout my history,
it settled on moments that were otherwise unremarkable like a fine dust,
a white ash,
a black night.

How could I have seen the last moments of his life in a field of blowing flowers?
His footsteps around a ladder at dusk?
A sign in the brief illumination of a tree at night?

I cannot go back and tell him what is to come.
I cannot grab him from the tracks or call him and tell him to go home.
I cannot photograph him resignedly,
tirelessly depicting the last years of a boy who will one day vanish from the world.

I can arrange my photographs looking for meaning,
finding only the indelible traces he left behind.
